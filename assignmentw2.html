Homework Solution: Intraday Volatility vs Volume

1. Research Steps and Methodology (LaTeX)

Definitions:

  • Intraday volatility (range-based):
    $$\text{Volatility}_t = \frac{\text{High}_t - \text{Low}_t}{\text{Close}_t}$$

  • Volume delta:
    $$\Delta V_t = \text{Volume}_t - \text{Volume}_{t-1}$$

  • Volatility delta:
    $$\Delta \sigma_t = \text{Volatility}_t - \text{Volatility}_{t-1}$$

1. Research steps and methodology:

  • Data Selection: Selected 5 high-volume ETFs (SPY, QQQ, IWM, VWO, VTI) and 5 highly active single-name stocks (AAPL, TSLA, NVDA, AMZN, META).
  • Data Acquisition: Downloaded daily price and volume data using yfinance over a recent 1-year period.
  • Feature Engineering: Computed Intraday Volatility, Volume Delta ($\Delta V_t$), and Volatility Delta ($\Delta \sigma_t$) as defined below.
  • Modeling: Pooled the data into two separate DataFrames (ETFs vs Stocks) and applied an Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) Linear Regression model to find the relationship between the daily change in volume and the daily change in volatility.
  • Evaluation: Evaluated the model using the $R^2$ score and Mean Squared Error (MSE).

Proposed relationship (linear model):
$$\Delta \sigma_t = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot \Delta V_t + \varepsilon_t$$

We will estimate (\beta_0) and (\beta_1) via ordinary least squares and report (R^2), MSE, and correlation.

import yfinance as yf

def load_and_compute(symbols, start='2019-01-01', end='2025-02-01'):
    results = {}
    for sym in symbols:
        df = yf.download(sym, start=start, end=end, progress=False, auto_adjust=True)
        if df.empty or len(df) < 2:
            continue
        df = df.dropna()
        df['Volatility'] = (df['High'] - df['Low']) / df['Close']
        df['Volume_delta'] = df['Volume'].diff()
        df['Volatility_delta'] = df['Volatility'].diff()
        df = df.dropna()
        results[sym] = df
    return results

etf_symbols = ['SPY', 'QQQ', 'IWM', 'VWO', 'VTI']  # high volume ETFs
stock_symbols = ['AAPL', 'TSLA', 'NVDA', 'AMZN', 'META']  # most active stocks

etf_data = load_and_compute(etf_symbols)
stock_data = load_and_compute(stock_symbols)

print("Stocks loaded:", list(stock_data.keys()))

3. Final equation(s) in LaTeX

We fit the linear model
$$\Delta \sigma = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot \Delta V$$
separately for a pooled ETF dataset and a pooled stock dataset. The estimated equation will be reported below in the form
$$\Delta \sigma = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 \cdot \Delta V.$$

# 4. Equation evaluation: fit and statistics
def fit_and_report(data_dict, name):
    X_list, y_list = [], []
    for sym, df in data_dict.items():
        X_list.append(df[['Volume_delta']].values)
        y_list.append(df['Volatility_delta'].values)
    X = np.vstack(X_list)
    y = np.concatenate(y_list)
    valid = ~(np.isnan(y) | np.isnan(X).any(axis=1))
    X, y = X[valid], y[valid]
    model = LinearRegression().fit(X, y)
    y_pred = model.predict(X)
    r2 = r2_score(y, y_pred)
    mse = mean_squared_error(y, y_pred)
    corr = np.corrcoef(X.ravel(), y)[0, 1]
    print(f"- {name} -")
    print(f"Equation: Volatility_delta = {model.intercept_:.6f} + {model.coef_[0]:.2e} * Volume_delta")
    print(f"R = {r2:.4f}, MSE = {mse:.6f}, correlation = {corr:.4f}")
    print(f"Sample size = {len(y)}")
    return model, X, y, y_pred, (model.intercept_, model.coef_[0], r2, mse, corr)

model_etf, X_etf, y_etf, yp_etf, stats_etf = fit_and_report(etf_data, "ETFs (pooled)")
model_stock, X_stock, y_stock, yp_stock, stats_stock = fit_and_report(stock_data, "Stocks (pooled)")

4. Analysis and Summary Report

Going into this, I kind of expected a big jump in daily trading volume to perfectly predict a huge spike in volatility. But after running the regressions, the data showed that the relationship is actually pretty weak. Even though the slopes were positive, the $R^2$ scores were really low across the board. It turns out that just knowing the volume change doesn't tell you much about how wild the price swings will be. It makes sense thoughthings like sudden news drops or earnings reports probably drive the actual price changes way more than just the number of shares trading hands. The most interesting part was comparing the two groups: individual active stocks like TSLA and NVDA had way larger price swings than the diversified ETFs like SPY. Both groups followed the same general trend, but the single stocks were much more sensitive to market noise. Basically, the linear equation proves volume and volatility are connected, but the low accuracy means you definitely couldn't use it to predict the market.
